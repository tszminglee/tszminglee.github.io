Towards the unity of movement: implications of verb movement in Cantonese (Dissertation)

Abstract:

Displacement (of linguistic expressions) is a ubiquitous phenomenon in natural language. In the generative tradition, displacement is modeled in terms of transformation, or more precisely, movement, which establishes dependencies among syntactic constituents in a phrase structure. This thesis probes the question regarding to what extent movement theories can be unified. Specifically, I ad- dress issues surrounding the debate of the distinction between head movement and phrase movement over the past few decades. The distinction presupposes that structural complexity of the moving element is correlated with its movement properties. The goal of this thesis is to show that this is an unwarranted assumption.

Based on a number of case studies on verb displacement phenomena in Cantonese, I attempt a unified theory of movement by abandoning the head/phrase distinction in movement theories. Particularly, I show (i) that verbs in Cantonese can undergo syntactic movement to the peripheral position of a sentence and is subject to general locality/minimality constraints on movement, and (ii) that their movement may affect semantic interpretation, leading to discourse effects and scope effects that are commonly observed in phrasal movement. I further argue, with evidence from linearization, that head movement and phrasal movement in Cantonese are subject to the same mechanism when determining the pronunciation of the movement chains.

These observations converge on the conclusion that the phrase structure status of syntactic constituents bears a minimal role in theorizing displacement phenomena in natural language. This thesis represents a minimalist pursuit of a unified theory movement.

Head movement: interpretations and locality conditions

I look at the interpretive effects of head movement. I uncover cases in Cantonese where overt head movement of aspectual verbs and modal verbs leads to scope enrichment. This lands support to the syntactic nature of head movement.

  • Movement of quantificational heads and scope enrichment

  • Movement of modal verbs and focus interpretation (with Ka-Fai Yip)

Language: Mandarin – book chapter | manuscript | proceedings-NACCL| handout-NACCL

I also look at the locality constraints of head movement. I advocate a reformulation of locality/minimality conditions on head movement based on featural interveners, instead of structural interveners.

Syntactic locality, and its flexible nature

Independent evidence reveals that requirements of syntactic locality may be more flexible than previously thought. They may interact with syntactic dependencies or can be deactivated or re-sized during the derivation. The empirical evidence comes from hyperraising structures in Cantonese and Vietnamese and VP fronting and ellipsis in Cantonese and Mandarin.

  • Hyper-raising, evidentiality, and phase deactivation (joint work with Ka-Fai Yip)

Language: Cantonese, Vietnamese – manuscript | proceedings-WCCFL 38 | handout-BCGL

  • VP movement, aspectual verbs, and a contextual approach to phasehood (joint work with Victor Junan Pan)

Language: Cantonese, Mandarin – handout-UConn

Temporal reference/ Genuine tenselessness

I work on temporal reference of languages without overt tense morphology such as Cantonese. I pursue a possibility that there is no genuine tense in Cantonese. Temporal reference is achieved via evaluation time shift. This approach makes a different sets of predictions compared to other tensed approaches. This is joint work with Roumi Pancheva and Maria Luisa Zubizarreta.

  • Encoding time in Cantonese

Modality: acceptability and optimality

I investigate the strength of modals as reflected on the difference between English must and should. I probe the question as to whether the strength difference is also reflected on possibility modals.

  • Modal strength in deontic modals, strong possibility and optimality

Linearization: verb doubling and discontinuous predicates

Verb movement has a non-uniform pattern of phonological realization: it can be pronounced twice, leading to a doubling effect, or it can be partially realized, leading to a discontinuous string. I suggest that the non-uniform pattern results from interactions between different phonological operations.

  • Doubling of verbs, subjects and objects, and Cyclic Linearization

Right dislocation, and its discourse effects

Cantonese allows elements to be dislocated to the position after sentence-final particles. I discuss the derivational path of such elements. I also propose a process of defocalization which is also found in other languages but how such process is grammatizalized varies from language to language.

  • Right dislocation and defocalization
